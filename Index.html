MARINE LIFE

INTRODUCTION

The plants, animals, and other creatures that inhabit the salty waters of estuaries along the coast or the acidic waters of seas and oceans are referred to as aquatic life, sea life, or marine life. Aquarium life has a fundamental impact on the planet's nature. Carbon is stored and oxygen is produced by marine organisms, primarily microorganisms. Some aquatic organisms even assist in the creation of new land, such as coral reef constructors, and they also help to shape and safeguard shorelines.There wouldn't be life on Earth without water. Because it can dissolve a wide range of substances, scientists have traditionally referred to water as the solvent of choice. It becomes the ideal setting for bacteria to thrive and chemical reactions.



JELLYFISH

ABOUT

Prior to the appearance of prehistoric animals, jellyfish have existed on Earth for millions of years. These jelly-like creatures, which can be observed in both deep and shallow seas, in addition to in coastlines, are found vibrating along on our ocean currents. Pink, yellow, blue, and purple jellyfish are among the vivid colors discovered between them; some are clear. Additionally, they can produce light—a phenomenon known as bioluminescence! No brain, heart, bones, or eyes are found in jellyfish. They are made up of tentacles containing tiny, stinging cells and a smooth, bag-like body. The stinging tentacles of these outstanding insects serve to paralyze or stun their prey, that they then eat.

Jellyfish Predators

Jellyfish don't have many enemies in the ocean because they're slimy and some can sting. However, a few animals still eat them. Sea turtles, big fish like sunfish, and some birds sometimes snack on jellyfish. Even some little sea slugs munch on them, and they even use jellyfish's stingy cells to protect themselves. Despite these predators, jellyfish populations can still grow fast because they make lots of babies and can live in different conditions. This can sometimes cause problems in the ocean and for us humans too.

Prey

Jellyfish primarily prey on small aquatic creatures like plankton, tiny fish, fish eggs, and other jellyfish. They catch their prey using their tentacles, which have special cells called nematocysts that release venom when touched. This venom helps jellyfish to immobilize or kill their prey. Some jellyfish species also eat zooplankton and small crustaceans. Despite being good at catching prey, jellyfish themselves don't have many predators because of their stinging tentacles and sometimes poisonous body.

Different Types of Jellyfish

COMB-Jelly

Despite its jelly-like appearance, the Comb Jelly is not a jellyfish,belongs to the phylum Ctenophora.Unlike jellyfish, comb jellies do not possess stinging cells. Instead,they capture prey using specialized adhesive cells called colloblasts.These cells secrete a sticky substance that ensnares small marine organisms.

BOX JELLYFISH

Chironex fleckeri, holds the title of being the largest of the box jellyfish. It commands attention in the waters it inhabits. Its distinguishing feature lies in its thick, bootlace-like tentacles that can stretch up to an impressive length of 10 feet. These long tentacles are armed with extremely venomous cells called nematocysts, making encounters with this species potentially fatal, of course, you are a leatherback sea turtle.

PORTUGUES-JELLYFISH

Get ready to encounter one of the ocean's most intriguing creatures, the Portuguese Man-of-War. Despite its name, this marine hydrozoan is not a true jellyfish but rather a colony of specialized organisms called zooids working together as a single entity.

MOON JELLYFISH

Moon Jellyfish, scientifically known as Aurelia aurita, are ethereal creatures that gracefully glide through the ocean. With their translucent and bell-shaped bodies, floating moons in the water. These jellyfish are frequently encountered in coastal regions globally and one can often spot sizable gatherings while snorkeling in Hawaii.

UPSIDE-DOWN JELLYFISH

Prepare to witness a jellyfish that defies gravity the Upside-Down Jellyfish. Unlike their drifting counterparts, these jellies prefer to settle on the seafloor, often inverting themselves and forming clusters that resemble soft coral. This unique behavior, sets it apart from other jellyfish species.

SEA-NETTLE JELLYFISH

The Sea Nettle is renowned for its vibrant and graceful appearance, trailing tentacles and a translucent bell adorned with beautiful red, or brown hues. Its long, flowing tentacles and vibrant coloration make it a true spectacle. Beware, for its sting can cause a painful reaction in humans.
